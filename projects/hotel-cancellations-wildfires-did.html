Causal Inference on Sparse Counts: A Poisson Difference-in-Differences Regression Tutorial on Hotel Bookings and Wildfires

← Back to Portfolio View on GitHub

Causal inference is the cornerstone of data-driven decision making. In both industry and research, Difference-in-Differences (DiD) is the workhorse methodology for estimating treatment effects when randomized experiments are unavailable. However, a major statistical blind spot arises when standard linear regressions are applied directly to sparse count data. When the target variable is a count (such as weekly bookings or cancellations) and contains a substantial fraction of zeros, standard Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) fails. It produces physically impossible negative predictions and suffers from severe specification bias if a log transformation, the classic log(Y + ε) workaround, is attempted.

In this tutorial, I walk through these exact econometric failures and present the solution, Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood (PPML) Difference-in-Differences. Using a real-world case study, the historic June 2017 Portuguese Mega-Wildfires and their impact on hotel demand from the famous Hotel Booking Demand Dataset, I explain how to build a robust causal pipeline. I also analyze denominator effects in cancellations, explain the multiplicative parallel trends assumption, and demonstrate how to extend this framework to multi-unit panels using modern econometric packages like etwfe and diff-diff.

Intuition & Data Source

The case study is built around a natural experiment. In June 2017, Portugal experienced the most devastating wildfires in its history, concentrated primarily in the central region of the country. This study leverages the Hotel Booking Demand Dataset (Antonio, de Almeida, and Nunes, 2019), which contains booking records from July 2015 to August 2017 for two hotels in Portugal:

  • Resort Hotel (Treated): Located in the Algarve, a southern coastal region heavily dependent on international tourism and vulnerable to wildfire demand shocks.
  • City Hotel (Control): Located in Lisbon, which was unaffected by the wildfires and serves as a baseline control.

The analysis focuses on the Groups market segment. Group bookings (tours, conferences, weddings) are highly seasonal and "lumpy" (booked and cancelled in large blocks). This makes them a perfect candidate for sparse count modeling since many weeks have exactly zero activity, interspersed with massive seasonal spikes.

I built a panel dataset using a weekly frequency (Monday to Monday). In this simple panel, the target variable total_bookings (demand) has a massive pile-up at zero, with 22.81% of all weeks recording exactly 0 group bookings.

Plotting the weekly booking demand over time shows the massive seasonal group demand spikes in the pre-wildfire period, followed by a sharp contraction for the Resort Hotel after the wildfire shock in week 24 (June 12, 2017).

The Causal Puzzle: Denominator & Cancellation Effects

A common assumption is that a disaster like a wildfire should increase cancellations. However, plotting the weekly cancellation trends for group bookings reveals a counter-intuitive phenomenon: following the wildfire, cancellations at the Resort Hotel dropped to absolute zero!

This is a classic demonstration of the denominator effect. A cancellation cannot exist without a booking. The wildfire was so devastating to local tourism that group demand completely collapsed. No new bookings were made for the summer and autumn arrival weeks. With zero bookings on the books, cancellations mechanically fell to absolute zero.

If I had analyzed cancellations in isolation without accounting for the denominator (overall booking demand), I might have concluded that the wildfire had a "beneficial" effect by reducing cancellations. This highlights the importance of using overall demand (total bookings) as the primary outcome, and keeping cancellations as a secondary metric to understand the underlying mechanics.

The Econometric Failures of Linear OLS

Failure 1: Physical Nonsense (Negative Predictions)

If I fit a standard linear OLS model on the simple panel to estimate the difference-in-differences effect:

Yit = β0 + β1 Treati + β2 Postt + θ (Treati × Postt) + εit

Where Treati is 1 for the Resort Hotel, Postt is 1 for post-wildfire weeks, and θ is the causal parameter. The linear model assumes a symmetric, normal distribution of residuals. Because count data is strictly non-negative, the OLS model predicts negative cancellation counts during off-peak winter weeks. In my simple panel, 5.26% of weeks yield physically impossible negative predictions (such as -2.84 × 10-14 cancellations).

Failure 2: The log(Y + ε) Landmine

To bypass negative predictions and stabilize variance, data scientists often transform the target variable using a log transformation. Since log(0) is undefined, they add an arbitrary constant ε (typically 1.0) and run OLS on the transformed outcome: log(Y + ε).

To demonstrate why this is a statistical landmine, I ran a sensitivity analysis fitting the log-linear OLS model for various choices of ε ∈ [1.0, 0.5, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001]. I tracked the estimated treatment coefficient (θ) and its standard errors:

offsets = [1.0, 0.5, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001]
coefficients = []
std_errors = []

for eps in offsets:
    panel_simple['y_log'] = np.log(panel_simple['cancellations'] + eps)
    model = smf.ols("y_log ~ treat + post + treat_post", data=panel_simple).fit()
    coefficients.append(model.params['treat_post'])
    std_errors.append(model.bse['treat_post'])

The results show that changing the arbitrary constant from 1.0 to 0.001 causes the estimated coefficient to shift from -0.8354 to -2.7667, a shift of over 300%! Because the estimated treatment effect is highly sensitive to an arbitrary tuning choice, log-linear OLS on sparse counts is statistically invalid.

The Poisson DiD Solution

Poisson regression models the conditional mean as an exponential function using a log link:

E[Yit | Treati, Postt] = exp(α + β1 Treati + β2 Postt + θ (Treati × Postt))

By modeling the counts natively, predictions remain strictly positive, and it handles zeros perfectly without requiring arbitrary transformations. Under the Poisson model, the parallel trends assumption shifts from a linear scale (constant absolute difference) to a multiplicative scale (constant percentage growth rate in the counterfactual). The treatment effect is interpreted using the Incidence Rate Ratio (IRR), calculated as exp(θ). The percentage change is (exp(θ) - 1) × 100.

I fitted the model using statsmodels' Generalized Linear Model (GLM) with a Poisson family. Importantly, I specified cov_type='HC0' to calculate robust standard errors. Under the Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood (PPML) framework, using robust standard errors ensures that the coefficient estimates remain consistent even if the data does not follow a strict Poisson distribution (for example, in the presence of overdispersion):

import statsmodels.api as sm
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# Fit Poisson DiD for Demand (total_bookings) using PPML
demand_model = smf.glm(
    "total_bookings ~ treat + post + treat_post", 
    data=panel_simple, 
    family=sm.families.Poisson()
).fit(cov_type='HC0')

# Fit Poisson DiD for Cancellations (cancellations)
cancellation_model = smf.glm(
    "cancellations ~ treat + post + treat_post", 
    data=panel_simple, 
    family=sm.families.Poisson()
).fit(cov_type='HC0')

Let's interpret the causal parameters estimated by the Poisson models:

  • Demand Model (total_bookings): The causal coefficient θ is -2.4290 (p = 0.004). The IRR is exp(-2.4290) = 0.0881. This means the wildfire shock caused a statistically significant 91.19% reduction in the rate of group bookings.
  • Cancellation Model (cancellations): The causal coefficient θ is -25.0145 (p = 0.000). The IRR is exp(-25.0145) = 0.0000, reflecting a complete 100% reduction in weekly cancellations, illustrating the denominator collapse.

Advanced Causal Inference: Extended Two-Way Fixed Effects

In modern applied econometrics, researchers frequently work with multi-unit panels (multiple cities, countries, or hotels) and staggered treatment times. In these settings, traditional Two-Way Fixed Effects (TWFE) models are biased if treatment effects are heterogeneous over time or across units.

To resolve this, Jeffrey Wooldridge (2021, 2023) developed the Extended Two-Way Fixed Effects (ETWFE) estimator. This is a nonlinear saturated DiD model that handles heterogeneous treatment effects across units and cohorts without introducing the biases of traditional TWFE. I implemented ETWFE on a multi-unit weekly panel (Online TA segment for top 5 countries) using overall demand (total_bookings) as the outcome variable:

from etwfe import etwfe

# Fit Wooldridge's saturated Poisson DiD model for demand
etwfe_model = etwfe(
    fml="total_bookings ~ 0",            # Outcome variable (no extra covariates)
    tvar="week_idx",                   # Time variable
    gvar="first_treated_week",         # Treatment cohort (when first treated)
    ivar="unit_id",                    # Unit Fixed Effect ID
    family="poisson",                  # Runs Poisson DiD
    data=panel_multi,
    vcov="iid"                         # Set covariance type

print(etwfe_model.emfx().round(4))

The aggregated treatment effect (emfx) from the saturated Poisson model yields a statistically significant drop of -9.2331 bookings per week (standard error 5.1383, p = 0.072), validating the demand contraction in a multi-unit setting.

Pre-Trend Diagnostics with diff-diff

The identifying assumption of Difference-in-Differences is that the treated and control groups share parallel trends in the pre-treatment period. I formally tested this assumption on the pre-treatment period (before week 102) using the diff-diff library:

import diff_diff as dd

pre_periods = list(panel_simple[panel_simple['week_idx'] < 102]['week_idx'].unique())
trends_res = dd.check_parallel_trends(
    data=panel_simple,
    outcome='cancellations',
    time='week_idx',
    treatment_group='treat',
    pre_periods=pre_periods
)

print(f"Difference in Slopes: {trends_res['trend_difference']:.4f} (p-value: {trends_res['p_value']:.4e})")
print(f"Parallel Trends Plausible? {trends_res['parallel_trends_plausible']}")

The test confirms that the difference in pre-treatment trends is statistically insignificant (slope difference = 0.1862, p = 0.4764), verifying that the parallel trends assumption is plausible.

Model Comparison & Key Takeaways

Below is a summary of the weekly group booking demand causal estimates across the three models:

Model Specification Outcome Variable Causal Parameter (θ) Std. Error P-Value Causal Interpretation
1. Standard OLS DiD total_bookings +10.5000 33.090 0.751 +10.5 bookings/week change (statistically insignificant)
2. Poisson DiD (PPML) total_bookings -2.4290 0.839 0.004 -91.19% change in rate of bookings (statistically significant)
3. ETWFE Poisson DiD total_bookings -9.2331 5.138 0.072 -9.23 bookings/week change (statistically significant at 10% level)

This comparison highlights the core takeaways of the analysis:

  1. OLS fails on sparse count data: The linear model's assumption of normal, symmetric residuals leads to physically impossible negative predictions during off-peak periods, and fails to identify the treatment effect (estimating a positive, noisy, and insignificant coefficient of +10.5).
  2. The log(Y + ε) transformation is a statistical landmine: Attempting to bypass OLS issues using a log transformation with an arbitrary offset is highly volatile. Changing the offset shifts the estimated treatment effect by over 300%, rendering it invalid.
  3. Poisson DiD (PPML) is robust: By natively modeling the exponential link and utilizing robust standard errors (HC0), Poisson DiD accommodates the zeros, yields a statistically significant 91.19% drop in booking demand, and remains consistent under overdispersion.
  4. Accounting for denominator effects is critical: Post-treatment cancellations fell to absolute zero because demand collapsed entirely. Looking at cancellations in isolation would lead to the incorrect conclusion that the wildfire was beneficial.
  5. Adopt modern panel estimators: Saturated cohort-time interaction estimators like Wooldridge's ETWFE protect the analysis from heterogeneous treatment biases in multi-unit panels, backing out a robust treatment effect of -9.23 bookings per week.
