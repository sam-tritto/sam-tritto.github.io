Introduction: Why Z-Scores Are a Data Analyst's Secret Weapon

The FIFA World Cup 2026 has kicked off, and the world is watching. Every pundit has an opinion: who's the best player, who stepped up when it mattered, who was a passenger riding a great team's coattails. But sports intuition alone can only take you so far. When you're looking at 1,092 players across 128 matches, spanning six different positions and two tournaments, you need a systematic way to separate signal from noise.

Enter the Z-Score - one of the most elegantly simple and widely applicable tools in the analyst's toolkit. At its heart, a z-score answers a single question: How far is this value from the average, measured in units of standard deviation? That deceptively simple answer unlocks a tremendous amount of analytical power. Z-scores let you compare apples to oranges, flatten skewed distributions into a common language, and surface the truly exceptional performers hiding in seas of noisy data. Whether you're a product analyst comparing conversion rates across user cohorts, a finance analyst evaluating fund returns across market cycles, or a sports scientist ranking athletes across positions - z-scores are almost always a good idea as a first move.

In this tutorial, we're going to build up the z-score concept from scratch, layer on peer-group standardization, and then combine it with a sophisticated Leave-One-Out (LOO) possession chain engine powered by StatsBomb open data. The result is a multi-metric composite ranking framework that can tell us not just who scored the most, but who was truly indispensable to their team's attacking system - and how that compares to every other player in their position. By the end, you'll have a reusable framework you can apply to any sport, any business domain, or any dataset where you need to compare entities across multiple metrics and peer groups.

Part 1 - Z-Scores from First Principles

The Formula

The z-score formula is:

Z = (x − μ) / σ

Where:

  • x is the raw value for our observation
  • μ (mu) is the mean of the reference population
  • σ (sigma) is the standard deviation of that same population

A z-score of 0 means "perfectly average." A z-score of +1.0 means "one standard deviation above average," which is better than roughly 84% of the population. A z-score of +2.0 puts you above about 97.7% of peers. Anything above +3.0 is genuinely elite - a once-in-a-generation performer by any measure.

Why Not Just Use Raw Numbers?

Imagine you're comparing the performance of two sales reps: Alice sold $450,000 last quarter; Bob sold $220,000. Simple - Alice wins, right? Not so fast. If Alice covers the Northeast (avg $380K/quarter, σ = $60K) and Bob covers the Mountain West (avg $180K/quarter, σ = $30K), then:

Z_Alice = (450,000 − 380,000) / 60,000 = +1.17
Z_Bob = (220,000 − 180,000) / 30,000 = +1.33

Bob is actually performing better relative to his peers, even though his raw number looks smaller. This is the core insight: raw numbers embed context that z-scores strip away, letting you make fair comparisons across very different distributions.

Part 2 - Grouped Z-Scores: When One Distribution Isn't Enough

The Apples-to-Oranges Problem in Soccer

A goalkeeper who makes 8 saves in a match is extraordinary. A center forward who makes 8 saves... is playing in goal for some reason. Position determines what a "good" value looks like for almost every metric. If you compute a single global z-score for offensive influence across all 1,092 players in our dataset, strikers will dominate the leaderboard - not because they're the best players in the tournament, but because their job description involves touching the ball near goal. A world-class center back who contributes 0.15 xG per 90 minutes to possession chains looks terrible in a global ranking. But compared to other center backs? He might be exceptional.

The solution is grouped z-scores: compute the mean and standard deviation within a positional peer group, and standardize against that:

Z_i = (x_i − μ_g) / σ_g

Where μ_g and σ_g are the mean and standard deviation of metric x within peer group g.

The Six Grouping Strategies

Our notebook implements six distinct grouping strategies, forming a spectrum from coarse to fine-grained peer definitions:

#Group KeyCohortsPurpose
1group_global1 (all players)Baseline - unfiltered ranking
2group_tier4 (GK / DEF / MID / FWD)Coarse positional split
3group_cohort7 (Goalkeeper, Center Back, Fullback, Defensive Mid, Central Mid, Winger, Forward)Recommended - best balance
4group_position_raw18 (StatsBomb raw positions)Maximum specificity
5group_tenure2 (First WC / Returning)Cross-tournament experience
6group_age3 (Young / Prime / Veteran)Age-adjusted peer comparison

Using multiple grouping strategies simultaneously lets us ask richer questions. The 7-cohort grouping (strategy #3) is the recommended sweet spot - granular enough to separate roles meaningfully, while keeping cohort sizes large enough that the standard deviation is stable and the z-scores are well-behaved.

The Small-Sample Safeguard

When a cohort is very small (fewer than 10 players), its sample standard deviation becomes unreliable - a single outlier can wildly inflate or deflate it, producing extreme z-scores that don't mean much. We defend against this with a pooled sigma fallback: if any cohort falls below the minimum size threshold, we replace its σ with a size-weighted average σ across all cohorts:

σ_pooled = Σ(σ_g × n_g) / Σ(n_g)

This is the same logic behind pooled variance in Student's t-test. Here's the relevant Python function from the notebook:

def compute_grouped_scores(df, value_col, group_col, min_cohort_size=10, invert=False):
    """
    Compute Z-Scores within each peer group.
    If invert=True, lower values of value_col will yield higher Z-scores.
    """
    z_col = f"{group_col}_{value_col.replace('_p90', '')}_zscore"
    result = df.copy()
    result[z_col] = np.nan

    # Per-group statistics
    gstats = (
        df.groupby(group_col, observed=True)[value_col]
        .agg(["mean", "std", "count"])
        .reset_index()
    )

    # Pooled sigma for small-sample fallback
    valid = gstats.dropna(subset=["std"])
    pooled_std = (valid["std"] * valid["count"]).sum() / valid["count"].sum()

    for _, gs in gstats.iterrows():
        grp  = gs[group_col]
        mu   = gs["mean"]
        sigma = gs["std"]
        n    = gs["count"]

        # Small-cohort fallback
        if n < min_cohort_size or pd.isna(sigma) or sigma == 0:
            sigma = pooled_std

        mask = result[group_col] == grp
        val_slice = result.loc[mask, value_col]

        z = (mu - val_slice) / sigma if invert else (val_slice - mu) / sigma
        result.loc[mask, z_col] = z

    return result

Note the invert=True flag: for defensive metrics like opponent xG conceded while on the pitch, a lower value is better, so we flip the sign so that elite defenders still get high positive z-scores.

Part 4 - The LOO Engine: Measuring Indispensability

Beyond Box Scores: Possession Chain Value

Traditional scouting metrics like goals, assists, and key passes reward only the last touch or two in an attacking sequence. A midfielder who plays a precise line-breaking pass to a winger who then beats a defender and picks out a striker for a tap-in gets... nothing in the box score. The striker gets the goal, the winger might get the assist. The midfielder who started it all is invisible.

Leave-One-Out analysis fixes this by asking: how much would this team's attack have suffered if this player hadn't been on the pitch? We do this at the possession chain level.

How Possession Chain LOO Works

  1. Identify valuable chains. A possession chain is a StatsBomb possession sequence - a continuous stretch of events owned by one team. We flag every chain that ends in at least one shot, and value it as the sum of shot_statsbomb_xg across all shots in that chain.
  2. Credit every participant. Any player from the possession team who had any event in that chain (a touch, a pass, even winning the ball back) is considered a chain contributor.
  3. LOO Influence Δ. Each player's offensive influence delta is simply the sum of chain xG values across every chain they participated in - the total xG that would have been "lost" from the team's attack if that player had been removed from every chain.

A key property: because multiple players share credit for the same chain, the sum of all LOO deltas in a team will exceed the team's actual xG. That's not a bug - it's the point. The sum measures system dependence: how many times over the team relies on its collective participants. A team where every player's LOO delta equals the team's total xG has maximum system dependence.

The engine also introduces a Possession Chain Value (PCV) metric that adds a small bonus for chain length:

PCV = chain_xg + 0.01 × event_count

This rewards players who sustain possession sequences, even in chains that don't end in shots - a nod to the ball-retention work that often underpins elite attacking play.

Defensive LOO

Players who disrupt opponent possession chains also get credit. Interceptions, blocks, clearances, ball recoveries, and duels during opponent possession are tagged as defensive disruption events. The defensive LOO delta is the total xG value of opponent chains that the player disrupted:

# Net delta: offensive influence minus defensive disruptions
result["influence_delta_net_xg"] = (
    result["influence_delta_off_xg"] - result["influence_delta_def_xg"]
)

# Per-90 normalization for all metrics
result["influence_delta_p90"] = (
    result["influence_delta_off_xg"] / result["minutes_played"] * 90
)

All metrics are normalized per 90 minutes using actual pitch time (computed from StatsBomb lineup stint timestamps), making them comparable across players who appeared in different amounts of playing time.

Part 5 - Feature Engineering: xG and xGD

Before the LOO engine runs, we build team-level xG and expected goal differential (xGD) for every match:

MetricFormula
team_xgΣ shot_statsbomb_xg for all shots by the team
opponent_xgThe opposing team's team_xg in the same match
team_xgdteam_xg − opponent_xg

xGD is the gold standard for measuring offensive/defensive efficiency in soccer - it correlates far more strongly with future results than actual goal differential because it strips out goalkeeper heroics and finishing luck. Across our 256 team-match records (128 matches × 2 teams), the average xGD is exactly 0.0 (by construction), with a standard deviation of 1.31 and a range from −4.15 to +4.15.

Part 6 - Tournament Aggregation and Group Assignment

Match-level metrics are aggregated to the tournament level: we sum raw deltas and total minutes across all of a player's matches in a tournament, then recompute per-90 from the tournament totals. This avoids the "one great game" problem - a player who was excellent in one match but poor in all others shouldn't rank above a player who was consistently good throughout a tournament.

Players with fewer than 90 total tournament minutes are filtered out - we need a meaningful sample to produce stable z-scores.

Then every player gets assigned to all six groupings simultaneously. The 7-cohort grouping uses a mapping from StatsBomb's 18 raw positions down to 7 cohorts:

A player's cohort is determined by their tournament-level primary position - the position they played the most total minutes at across all matches in the tournament. This prevents short-stint position changes from contaminating a player's peer group.

Part 7 - The Composite Score: Position-Aware Weighted Z-Scores

With six separate z-score series computed for each of three raw metrics (offensive LOO, defensive LOO, defensive disruptions) across six grouping strategies, the next step is combining them into a single, interpretable composite ranking.

We use a position-aware weighting scheme from the 7-cohort grouping:

CohortOffensive weightOpponent xG Conceded weightDisruptions weight
Forward0.700.150.15
Winger0.700.150.15

This encoding reflects positional responsibility: forwards are primarily judged on their offensive chain contribution; goalkeepers are judged entirely on whether the team concedes xG while they're between the posts; central defenders split their weight between avoiding conceded xG and active disruptions. The composite formula is:

Composite_Z = w_off × Z_off + w_conc × Z_conc + w_disrupt × Z_disrupt

Three variants of the composite are computed - using the original LOO delta, the xGD LOO net delta, and the PCV LOO delta - enabling comparison across modeling philosophies.

Part 9 - Risers and Fallers: The Global vs. Cohort Ranking Shift

The notebook computes explicit "riser" and "faller" rankings - players whose composite z-score changes most dramatically when switching from global to cohort standardization:

Risers are typically defenders and deep-lying midfielders. Their raw LOO deltas are modest in absolute terms but exceptional relative to their positional peers. A center back who reads the game impeccably, disrupts opponent chains, and still contributes to ball circulation will shoot up the cohort rankings even though a global ranking would bury him behind every forward.

Fallers are often forwards with high raw influence deltas that look unremarkable once measured against other forwards doing the same job. A striker with a 1.0 xG per 90 influence delta might be solidly average within the Forward cohort even though they rank highly globally.

This is the most practically useful output for scouts and analysts: a ranked list of who's genuinely over-performing their positional average, regardless of where their position sits in the raw metric hierarchy.

Key Takeaways

  1. Z-scores are a universal normalization tool. Any time you're comparing values across groups with different baselines or scales, standardize within the relevant peer group before comparing.
  2. Grouping strategy is a design decision, not a constant. Coarser groupings (4-tier) are more robust to small samples; finer groupings (18 raw positions) are more position-specific but need the pooled-σ safeguard.
  3. LOO analysis surfaces invisible contributors. Players who don't score or assist can still be the most indispensable player in a possession system - the LOO framework makes that visible.
  4. Per-90 normalization is essential. Raw totals reward players with more playing time. Per-90 reveals rate of contribution independent of opportunity.
  5. Multiple composite models are better than one. The agreement between the original LOO, xGD LOO, and PCV LOO models adds confidence; the disagreements highlight where methodology assumptions matter most.
  6. The framework is domain-agnostic. Replace "possession chains" with "sales cycles," "patient care episodes," or "code commits" and the grouped z-score composite pattern applies equally well.
