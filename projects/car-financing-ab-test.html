Background

It will be important to first understand how the company operates and generates their margins. The company sells cars to customers and also offers loans for these cars. Each loan will have a different APR depending on the FICO score bucket the customer falls into, and there is a fixed $1000 vehicle margin on each car sale. They then take these loans and resell them to banks and other companies thus generating a finance margin. These finance margins differ depending on the customer’s FICO score. The company would like to test lowering the APR by 1.0 point for both the 700 and 600 FICO score customers. They are mainly concerned with the conversion rates associated with their loan process and would like to run an A/B test to determine if the lower APR rates are an effective way to increase their conversion rates and margins.

A/B Test Overall Structure

The randomized test has been structured to run two different tests side by side for each variable FICO score. These tests will be independent themselves as each user can only be bucketed into one FICO score and the tests will allocate an approximately equal amount of samples into each subgroup. Essentially the structure will be to compare A/B and C/D. Also, this test has been structured where each sample is comprised only of users on their first interaction to the website, again to assure independence. 

The success metric was chosen to be the conversion rate of the customers who signed a loan when presented with an offer. Total margin per app was established as a guardrail metric to monitor any adverse or unwanted behaviors due to the increased conversions. Other metrics such as finance margin per app and vehicle margin per app were also monitored for context. 

After consulting with the business leaders and looking at historical data from past tests, a minimum detectable effect (MDE) of 0.06 was decided. The standard 0.05 alpha significance value and 0.8 statistical power value were also decided. For the guardrail metric a non-inferiority margin was decided to be a loss of $5 on the total margin per app. 

Bonferroni Correction

To help protect against a Type-1 error and control the family-wise error rate I applied a Bonferroni Correction to my chosen alpha value of 0.05. My confidence intervals have also been adjusted to accommodate for this corrected alpha. Rather than 95% confidence intervals I will use 97.5% confidence intervals.

Success Metric Hypothesis Test

In an A/B test much of the analysis revolves around comparing statistics such as the means of each group. However for conversions, which can take a value of 1 or 0, these actually come from a Bernoulli distribution. Since my dependent (0 and 1 conversions) and independent variables (APRs of 5.0 and 4.0 etc… ) are both categorical, I can use the Chi-Squared test to compare groups after setting up a contingency table. Here I use the chi2_contingency() function from the scipylibrary. I was able to reject the null hypothesis that lowering the APR will not effect the conversion rate. This is good news for this experiment, alluding to the fact that a drop in APR by 1.0 point could result in a higher conversion rate. To determine whether the drop in APR will increase or decrease conversions will be reveled by comparing each groups conversion rate in a 97.5% confidence interval shortly. 

The Chi-Squared test’s only two assumptions are that the observations are independent and that the sample sizes should not be too small. It doesn’t care about normality or variance, which is wonderful for our purposes here.

Guardrail Metric

When looking a the guardrail metric of Total Margin per App for both FICO score bucket subgroups we can easily see a decrease or negative effect. For the FICO 600 group there is only a small $3.61 decrease, however for the FICO 700 the decrease is greater at $21.75. This is a greater decrease than our non-inferiority margin of $5. This is not good news for our experiment. Right now I’m tempted to not recommend the drop in APR for the 700 group, but consider it for the 600 group. I’ll make my final decision after I take into consideration the effect’s size and expected value of each group next.

This has highlighted the importance of a using a guardrail metric. In this instance, I would have to go back to the business mangers and ask them if an increase in conversion rates would be worth a decrease in total margin per app. I’m working under the assumption that they would say it would not be worthwhile, though there are instances where this would actually be acceptable — it is therefore extremely important to highlight this for them.

Effects Size

Next I ran Cohen’s d to calculate the effect size of lowering the APR on conversions. I found that in both subgroups there was only a small effects size. This will solidify my decision to not recommend a drop in APR for both groups.

# function to calculate Cohen's d for independent samples

defcohend(d1, d2):

# calculate the size of samples

n1, n2 =len(d1), len(d2)

# calculate the variance of the samples

s1, s2 =np.var(d1, ddof=1), np.var(d2, ddof=1)

# calculate the pooled standard deviation

s =np.sqrt(((n1 -1) *s1 +(n2 -1) *s2) /(n1 +n2 -2))

# calculate the means of the samples

u1, u2 =np.mean(d1), np.mean(d2)

# calculate the effect size

return(u1 -u2) /s

# calculate cohen's d

d =cohend(test_600_conv, control_600_conv)

print('Cohens d: %.3f'%d)

print('\n')

ifd < 0.01:

print("Very small effect size")

elifd < 0.2:

print("Small effect size")

elifd < 0.5:

print("Medium effect size")

elifd < 0.8:

print("Large effect size")

elifd < 1.2:

print("Very Large effect size")

else:

print("Huge effect size")

Summary of A/B Test

For the FICO score 600 subgroup, I found the conversion uplift to be 3.4 percentage points with a p-value of 0.00004296. Therefore I can reject the null hypothesis that a decrease in APR by 1.0 point will not effect the conversion rate and conclude that a decrease in APR by 1.0 point will likely increase conversions.

Similarly for the FICO score 700 subgroup, I found the conversion uplift to be 3.28 percentage points with a p-value of 0.00026661. Therefore I can reject the null hypothesis that a decrease in APR by 1.0 point will not effect the conversion rate and conclude that a decrease in APR by 1.0 point will likely increase conversions. 

Findings

Although a decrease in APR brought in a higher number of sales and drove up the conversion rates in both FICO buckets, it unfortunately also lead to a decrease in total margin, and thus total margin per app for both groups as well.

Because of the increase in conversion rate there was also an increase of 333 cars sold when comparing test to control groups.

While there is a slight increase in vehicle margin per app for a decrease in APR, it is far outweighted by the inverse effect on the finance margin.

The expected value of a sale in the control group, $760.35, was higher than in the test group, $734.95, by $25.40.

For a drop of 1.0 point in APR there is a loss of $3.65 in total margin per app for the 600 FICO score group.

For a drop of 1.0 point in APR there is a loss of $21.75 in total margin per app for the 700 FICO score group.

Despite the large loss in total margins per app, the 700 bucket had a much smaller loss in total margin overall likely due to the increase in sales and overall higher conversion rates.

On top of this in both subgroups, the effects size of APR on conversions was found to be small, by Cohen’s d. 

Conclusions

Perhaps it is worth considering trying to increase the vehicle margin per sale in this 700 group to make up for the finance margin, but this might deter the customer who would in turn then be spending more out of pocket.

My recommendation would be then for the business leaders to try to renegotiate the vehicle’s finance margins with the companies purchasing these car loans toward a higher margin, especially for the 700 FICO score group. With an increase in loans for sale due to the increase in conversions brought in by the new low APR, the buyers might be willing to renegotiate.

By using expected value, I will demonstrate that if we are able to negotiate a $100 dollar increase in finance margins for both FICO buckets, a lower APR will become sustainable.

Hopefully then we can re-run this A/B test to see an increase in Total Margin per App as well as Total Conversions.
