The 20-Hour Benefit Cliff: Bayesian Causal Inference on Hawaii's Labor Market Distortions

← Back to Portfolio View on GitHub

Labor regulations with binary thresholds often create unintended economic incentives. In Hawaii, a unique and long-standing law provides a textbook case: the Prepaid Health Care Act of 1974. It mandates that employers must provide comprehensive healthcare coverage to any employee who works 20 hours or more per week. Because providing health insurance represents a substantial fixed cost per worker, this law creates a sharp compliance cost curve, commonly referred to as a benefit cliff. To avoid triggering this mandate, employers may intentionally cap part-time shifts at exactly 19 hours. This behavioral response is known as labor bunching.

I built a Bayesian causal inference pipeline to estimate how much Hawaii's labor hour distribution bunches at 19 hours compared to a counterfactual distribution. I evaluated two different estimators implemented in PyMC to construct this baseline: a traditional Poisson polynomial bunching regression and a Distributional Synthetic Control model. This tutorial focuses on the unique statistical challenges of this economic setup, specifically how self-reported survey rounding behaviors (the "survey heap effect") can cause standard bunching estimators to fail, and how Distributional Synthetic Control resolves this issue to isolate the true causal impact.

The Data Source and Preprocessing Pipeline

To analyze this policy effect, I pooled worker-level microdata from the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) Current Population Survey (CPS) Monthly Basic Files spanning 2021 to 2023. The CPS is a monthly household survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The dataset contains detailed records of weekly hours worked, geographic region, month-in-sample, class of worker, and industry classifications.

Because the NBER data servers utilize CDNs that block command-line script downloads, I structured a local ingestion script to pool the monthly raw CSV-formatted zip archives. I restricted the sample to a panel of tourism-dependent and high-cost-of-living states: Hawaii (Treatment) and four control states (California, Nevada, Florida, Alaska) that do not share the 20-hour healthcare mandate. The preprocessing pipeline filters the microdata to ensure structural comparability across the units:

  • Private Sector Focus: Filtered to include only private sector workers (excluding self-employed and public sector employees) to isolate corporate shift-scheduling behavior.
  • Month-in-Sample (MIS) 1 restriction: Kept only workers in their first month of the survey to eliminate longitudinal sample overlap and ensure independent observations.
  • Part-Time Range: Restricted the sample to workers reporting between 10 and 30 hours per week to focus on the policy-distorted part-time region.

After filtering, the final pooled dataset contains 26,306 individual worker records across the five states.

The Specific Causal Challenge: The Survey Heap Effect

In self-reported labor surveys, respondents exhibit a strong cognitive bias toward rounding their working hours to multiples of five (10, 15, 20, 25, 30 hours). In the CPS data, this creates massive spikes at 15, 20, and 25 hours, even in states without any labor threshold regulations. This is known as the survey heap effect.

This rounding behavior poses a severe threat to causal identification:

  1. Bunching Failure: Standard polynomial bunching models fit a smooth curve to the treated state's running variable (hours worked), omitting the region near the threshold (hours 18 to 23). Because the model expects a smooth progression, it expects a high counterfactual count at 19 hours due to the adjacent spike at 20. When it compares the actual count at 19 hours to this inflated curve, it under-predicts the baseline and can falsely conclude there is a labor deficit at 19 hours, completely missing the policy bunching.
  2. Synthetic Control Solution: Distributional Synthetic Control constructs the counterfactual as a weighted average of donor states. Because CA, NV, FL, and AK share the exact same cognitive rounding biases, their distributions also contain natural heaps at 15 and 20 hours. By fitting the weights on hours outside the policy-affected window, the synthetic counterfactual naturally expects low counts at 19 and a massive heap at 20, cleanly isolating the true policy-induced bunching in Hawaii.

Modeling Choices and PyMC Implementation

1. Bayesian Bunching (Poisson Regression)

The standard bunching estimator models the worker counts at each hour interval using a Poisson likelihood. The expected log-count is modeled as a flexible polynomial of the running variable (hours worked):

log(λh) = ∑k=0K βk hk

To avoid numerical overflow during MCMC sampling when calculating high-order polynomial powers, I mean-centered and scaled the hours variable. I specified regularizing Normal priors on the coefficients to prevent overfitting, and ran the PyMC sampler excluding the distorted range [18, 23]:

def fit_bayesian_bunching(hours_counts, exclude_range=(18, 23), poly_degree=5):
    h = hours_counts.index.values.astype(float)
    y = hours_counts.values
    
    # Define mask for non-excluded region
    mask = (h < exclude_range[0]) | (h > exclude_range[1])
    
    # Scale running variable to prevent numerical overflow in polynomial powers
    h_scaled = (h - h.mean()) / h.std()
    X = np.column_stack([h_scaled**k for k in range(poly_degree + 1)])
    
    X_fit = X[mask]
    y_fit = y[mask]
    
    with pm.Model() as bunching_model:
        # Priors for polynomial coefficients (tight priors on higher terms)
        beta = pm.Normal("beta", mu=0, sigma=10, shape=poly_degree + 1)
        
        # Expected counts (log link)
        log_mu = pm.math.dot(X_fit, beta)
        mu = pm.math.exp(log_mu)
        
        # Likelihood
        pm.Poisson("obs", mu=mu, observed=y_fit)
        
        # MCMC Sampling
        idata = pm.sample(draws=1000, tune=1000, target_accept=0.95, random_seed=42)
        
    beta_samples = az.extract(idata, var_names="beta").values.T
    lambda_pred = np.exp(np.dot(X, beta_samples.T))
    return idata, lambda_pred

2. Bayesian Distributional Synthetic Control

Distributional Synthetic Control models Hawaii's counterfactual distribution as a weighted combination of the donor states' distributions:

YHI, h ≈ ∑d wd Yd, h

The weights must be non-negative and sum to 1. To enforce these constraints natively without resorting to ad-hoc normalization, I modeled the weight vector in PyMC using a Dirichlet prior. The model is fit by excluding the policy-affected window [15, 24]:

def fit_bayesian_synthetic_control(hi_dist, donor_dists, exclude_range=(15, 24)):
    hours = hi_dist.index.values
    y_hi = hi_dist.values
    X_donors = donor_dists.values  # Shape: (len(hours), n_donors)
    n_donors = X_donors.shape[1]
    
    # Mask to exclude the policy-affected range
    mask = (hours < exclude_range[0]) | (hours > exclude_range[1])
    y_hi_fit = y_hi[mask]
    X_donors_fit = X_donors[mask]
    
    with pm.Model() as sc_model:
        # Dirichlet prior naturally guarantees that weights are non-negative and sum to 1
        w = pm.Dirichlet("w", a=np.ones(n_donors))
        
        # Expected value in the non-excluded region
        mu = pm.math.dot(X_donors_fit, w)
        
        # Likelihood noise parameter
        sigma = pm.HalfNormal("sigma", sigma=0.05)
        
        # Likelihood
        pm.Normal("obs", mu=mu, sigma=sigma, observed=y_hi_fit)
        
        # MCMC Sampling
        idata = pm.sample(draws=1000, tune=1000, target_accept=0.95, random_seed=42)
        
    w_samples = az.extract(idata, var_names="w").values.T
    synthetic_hi_pred = np.dot(X_donors, w_samples.T)
    return idata, synthetic_hi_pred

Quantitative Causal Impact Results

Comparing the actual Hawaii distribution against the posterior predictive distributions of our Synthetic Hawaii baseline yields the following causal estimates:

20–23 Hours (Mandate Deficit) 25.80% 29.87% (95% CI: 28.88% – 30.97%) -4.07% (95% CI: -5.17% – -3.08%)

The results show a massive 10.6x relative excess of workers scheduled for exactly 19 hours per week in Hawaii compared to the synthetic baseline. This is accompanied by a 4.07% labor deficit in the 20 to 23 hours range. This empirical evidence suggests that employers are systematically capping part-time work schedules at exactly 19 hours to avoid paying mandatory employee healthcare premiums.

Policy Implications & Lessons Learned

This causal analysis highlights a key lesson for public policy design. While the Prepaid Health Care Act successfully provides health insurance to full-time employees, the rigid binary threshold at 20 hours creates severe labor market distortions. Employers respond to the steep compliance cost curve by limiting shifts, which restricts the earning potential and hours of part-time workers who might otherwise choose to work 20-23 hours.

To eliminate this scheduling distortion, policymakers should design graduated subsidies or prorated employer contribution requirements. By smoothing the compliance cost curve, employers can schedule labor based on consumer demand rather than regulatory thresholds, allowing part-time employees to work their preferred schedules.
